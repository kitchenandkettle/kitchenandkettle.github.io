How to Make a Simple Herbal Salve

One recipe, three ingredients, no special equipment. Calendula salve for dry hands, cracked skin, and kitchen burns.

An herbal salve is just herb-infused oil thickened with a little beeswax — solid at room temperature, melting on contact with skin. It's the simplest thing you can make in home skincare, and one of the most useful. A good salve replaces hand cream, lip balm, and burn ointment in one small jar.

The recipe below uses calendula — the same marigold-family flower that grows in half the vegetable gardens in the country. It's one of the best-documented herbs for skin healing, and it's gentle enough for anyone. You can substitute other herbs once you know the method.

What you need

  • Dried calendula flowers. About half a cup, loosely packed. You can buy them dried or grow and dry your own — calendula is one of the easiest flowers to grow from seed.
  • A carrier oil. One cup. Olive oil works perfectly and is probably in your kitchen right now. Sweet almond oil is lighter and absorbs faster. Jojoba oil is the closest to human sebum and ideal for facial use, but more expensive. Don't use coconut oil — it goes solid at cool room temperature and won't infuse well.
  • Beeswax. About two tablespoons of pellets or grated beeswax per cup of infused oil. This is what sets the salve. More beeswax makes a firmer salve; less makes a softer one. Two tablespoons per cup gives you a balm that's solid in the jar but softens instantly on fingertips.
  • A clean jar. A small glass jar — two or four ounces. An old spice jar or a small mason jar works. Wash it well and dry it completely.

How to make it

  1. Infuse the oil. Place the dried calendula flowers in a clean, dry jar. Pour the carrier oil over them until they're fully covered by about an inch of oil. Seal the jar and place it in a warm spot — a sunny windowsill works — for two to four weeks. Shake gently every few days. The oil will turn a deep golden color. When it smells faintly herbal and earthy, it's ready.
  2. Strain the oil. Line a fine-mesh strainer with cheesecloth or a clean cotton cloth. Pour the oil through into a measuring cup. Squeeze the cloth gently to get every drop. You should have roughly one cup of infused oil. If any plant material got through, strain again — plant bits in your salve will mold.
  3. Melt in the beeswax. Pour the infused oil into a small heatproof bowl or a double boiler. Add the beeswax. Set the bowl over a pot of simmering water — the water shouldn't touch the bowl. Stir occasionally until the beeswax is fully melted and the mixture is clear. This takes about five minutes.
  4. Test the consistency. Dip a spoon into the hot oil mixture and set it in the freezer for two minutes. If the cooled salve is too soft, add a little more beeswax and melt again. If it's too firm, add a splash more oil. Two tablespoons per cup is the sweet spot for most uses.
  5. Pour and set. Pour the hot liquid into your clean jar. Let it cool undisturbed at room temperature — about an hour. It'll turn opaque and solid as it cools. Cap it and label it with the date and what's in it. A jar of calendula salve keeps for about a year in a cool, dark place.
Fast infusion, no waiting: If you want calendula salve today, use the quick method. Place the dried flowers and oil in a heatproof bowl over simmering water and warm gently for two to three hours. Don't let it get hot enough to fry the herbs — keep it at a bare simmer. Strain and proceed as above. The slow infusion gives a slightly richer result, but the fast one works fine.

What it's good for

Calendula salve is for dry, cracked, or irritated skin. Kitchen hands that are constantly wet and dry. Knuckle cracks from cold weather. Minor burns from reaching into the oven. Scrapes and scratches. Chapped lips. The backs of heels. It's not a miracle — it won't heal a deep wound or cure eczema — but for everyday skin repair, it's better than most things in a tube.

Honest note: If you have a skin condition — eczema, psoriasis, chronic dermatitis — herbal salves can help or they can make things worse depending on what's triggering you. Calendula is one of the gentlest herbs available and is safe for most people, but if you react to plants in the daisy family (ragweed, chamomile, arnica), skip calendula. Plantain leaf infused in oil is a safe alternative for nearly everyone. Test a small patch of skin before slathering anything new on an active flare.
