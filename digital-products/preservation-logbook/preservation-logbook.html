How to use this logbook

The point of preserving food is to eat it later. This logbook makes sure "later" actually happens — and that you remember what worked so you can do it again.

What's inside

  1. Canning log — Record every batch. Recipe, date, yield, processing method, and how it turned out when you opened it.
  2. Fermentation tracker — Start date, ingredients, salt ratio, tasting notes. Ferments change over time — track the journey.
  3. Dehydrating notes — What you dried, how you prepped it, temperature, time, and whether the result was worth repeating.
  4. Seasonal produce guide — What's in season when, and what to do with it. Reference pages, not blank templates.

When to use each section

  • During preserving season (summer/fall): Fill out logs as you go. Don't wait — you'll forget details.
  • When you open a jar (all year): Go back and add notes. Was the texture good? Too salty? Perfect? This is the most valuable data.
  • Before next year's season: Review last year's logs. Double the pickles, skip the pear butter, try that new hot sauce recipe.

The notes column is the most important field on every page. "Tasted flat — add more vinegar next time" is worth more than any recipe. Write down what actually happened.

A note on safety

This logbook assumes you already know safe canning and fermentation practices. If you're new to preserving, consult tested recipes and safety guidelines (USDA, NCHFP, or a reputable modern preserving book) before filling these pages.

The processing time and method fields exist because they matter for safety. Don't skip them. Botulism is rare but real — follow tested recipes.

A note on preserving

Preserving food isn't about stockpiling for the apocalypse. It's about capturing a moment of abundance — the week tomatoes are perfect, the month apples are falling off the tree — and spreading that abundance across the rest of the year.

Your grandmother's generation did this because they had to. You're doing it because you want to. That's a privilege. Enjoy it.

Some years you'll put up fifty jars of peaches and feel like a genius. Other years you'll preserve exactly nothing and eat takeout all August. Both years are fine. The logbook is here when you want it — not as an obligation.
