Eating With the Seasons — Without Overthinking It

What's actually in season when, why it matters, and how to build a calendar that fits your climate.

Seasonal eating sounds noble. In practice it means standing in the produce aisle in February staring at tomatoes from Mexico wondering if you're supposed to feel guilty. The truth is simpler: seasonal eating isn't about purity. It's about knowing when things are at their best — and their cheapest — so you can plan around it.

A strawberry in June tastes better and costs less than a strawberry in January. That's the whole idea. If you want strawberries in January, freeze them in June. The calendar isn't a set of rules — it's a tool for knowing what's coming and when to act.

What's actually in season, and when

This varies by region, but the broad pattern holds for most of North America. Adjust by about two weeks for every few hundred miles north or south of the middle of the country.

Spring (March–May). Greens, greens, and more greens. Lettuce, spinach, arugula, asparagus, peas, radishes, green onions, rhubarb. Early strawberries at the tail end. This is the season of tender things that don't store well — eat them fresh, don't try to preserve them. Spring is also when hens start laying heavily again after the winter slowdown.

Summer (June–August). Everything. Tomatoes, peppers, eggplant, zucchini, cucumbers, green beans, corn, stone fruit (peaches, plums, cherries), berries, melons. This is the season of abundance and also the season of panic — too much at once. Summer is for canning tomatoes, freezing berries, pickling cucumbers, and drying herbs. Everything you'll eat in winter gets preserved now.

Fall (September–November). The heavy harvest. Winter squash, pumpkins, potatoes, sweet potatoes, apples, pears, Brussels sprouts, cabbage, kale, carrots, beets, turnips. These are the storage crops — they'll keep for months in a cool place with no processing at all. Fall is also when you put up the last of the summer garden and transition to root-cellar mode.

Winter (December–February). Storage crops, citrus, and whatever you preserved. This is the lean season — and the reason you spent August canning tomatoes. Winter eating is about what you planned for. Squash, potatoes, onions, apples from the cellar. Frozen berries and green beans. Canned tomatoes and pickles. Fresh citrus from warmer regions. Meat birds if you raise them — winter is traditional butchering time.

Your climate, your calendar. A seasonal eating guide written in Vermont won't match your garden in Georgia. Tomatoes that come in July up north are done by June in the South. The calendar that matters is the one you build from your own observations over a year or two. The guide linked below gives you the framework and lets you fill in your own dates.

Why bother?

Three concrete reasons, none of them moral:

  1. Cost. In-season produce can be half the price of off-season — or less, if you grow it or buy in bulk at peak.
  2. Taste. A tomato ripened on the vine in August sun tastes completely different from one picked green and gassed red in a truck. Same goes for strawberries, peaches, corn, and anything else with a short peak window.
  3. Preservation makes sense when it's cheap. Freezing $2-a-flat strawberries in June is smart. Freezing $5-a-pint strawberries in January is expensive and they won't taste as good anyway.
Honest note: You don't have to eat 100% seasonally. Buy the lime and the avocado and the olive oil. Those things were never local to you and never will be. Seasonal eating is about the things that do grow near you — knowing when they peak so you can catch the wave. If you only preserve one thing a year, make it tomatoes. Everything else is bonus.
