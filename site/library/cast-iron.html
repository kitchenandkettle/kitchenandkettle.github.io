Cast Iron Care

Season it right. Clean it simply. Keep it forever.

Cast iron has a reputation for being fussy. It isn't. Your grandmother's pan survived decades of daily use without YouTube tutorials, specialty oils, or a single drop of dish soap anxiety. Cast iron needs three things: season it, clean it, cook with it. Everything else is optional.

Why cast iron works

Seasoning isn't a coating — it's polymerized oil bonded to the iron at a molecular level. When you heat oil past its smoke point on the surface of the pan, the fat molecules cross-link into a hard, slick, non-stick layer. This layer builds over time with use. Every time you cook with oil, you're adding to it.

Honest note: A brand-new cast iron pan won't perform like a thirty-year-old one. The non-stick surface comes from layers of seasoning built up through cooking. Be patient. It gets better every time you use it.

How to season a pan

You can season a new pan, restore a thrift-store find, or refresh a pan that's lost its seasoning. The method is the same.

  1. Clean it. Scrub the pan with hot water and mild soap. Yes, soap. Dry thoroughly with a towel, then put it on low heat for a minute to evaporate any remaining moisture.
  2. Oil it. Pour a small amount of high-smoke-point oil into the pan — canola, grapeseed, or vegetable oil all work. Rub it over every surface with a paper towel: inside, outside, handle. Then take a clean paper towel and wipe it off like you made a mistake. The pan should look dry. The oil layer you want is microscopic.
  3. Heat it. Place the pan upside down in a cold oven. Set the oven to 450°F (230°C). Put a sheet of foil on the rack below to catch any drips. Once the oven reaches temperature, set a timer for one hour. Turn off the oven and let the pan cool inside.

Repeat this process 2–3 times for a new or stripped pan. For a pan that just needs a refresh, once is enough.

What oil to use: Canola, grapeseed, vegetable, or flaxseed oil all polymerize well. Olive oil and butter have lower smoke points and can leave a sticky residue. Avoid them for seasoning.

Daily cleaning

Cleaning cast iron is simpler than the internet would have you believe.

  1. Clean it while it's warm. After cooking, let the pan cool for a few minutes — not cold, just not screaming hot. Warm food residue releases more easily.
  2. Hot water and a scrub. Use hot water and a stiff brush or chainmail scrubber. For stuck-on bits, a paste of coarse salt and a little water makes an effective abrasive that won't strip seasoning.
  3. Dry it completely. Towel dry, then put it on the stove over low heat for a minute. Water is the enemy of cast iron — not because it ruins the pan, but because it causes rust.
  4. Light coat of oil. While the pan is still warm, rub a few drops of oil into the cooking surface with a paper towel. This is maintenance, not a full seasoning cycle.

The do's and don'ts

Do:

  • Use metal utensils. A metal spatula won't hurt seasoning and actually helps smooth the surface over time.
  • Preheat the pan before adding food. Cast iron heats slowly but holds heat incredibly well.
  • Cook acidic foods (tomatoes, wine, vinegar) in a well-seasoned pan. Brief contact is fine. Long simmers can strip young seasoning.

Don't:

  • Soak the pan in water. Rust will form.
  • Put it in the dishwasher. Ever.
  • Use steel wool on a well-seasoned pan. Chainmail is gentler. Save steel wool for stripping a pan down for re-seasoning.
  • Store with the lid on. Cast iron needs airflow. If you stack pans, put a paper towel between them.

Restoring a neglected pan

Thrift store finds and garage-sale pans often come with rust or flaking seasoning. They're almost always salvageable.

  1. Strip it. Scrub with steel wool and hot water until you're down to bare iron. For heavy rust or thick crud, a 50/50 white vinegar and water soak (30 minutes max — vinegar eats iron) will loosen things up.
  2. Dry immediately. Rust forms fast on bare iron. Dry thoroughly and get oil on it within minutes.
  3. Season from scratch. Follow the seasoning method above, but do it 3–4 times. The first coat will look patchy. That's normal. Keep going.
Honest note: Not every pan is worth saving. If the cooking surface is deeply pitted, warped, or cracked, it's done. Use it as a decoration or recycle it.

What to cook in cast iron

Cast iron excels at high-heat cooking: searing steaks, frying chicken, baking cornbread, roasting vegetables. It's also your best pan for anything that needs to go from stovetop to oven — frittatas, Dutch babies, skillet cookies.

What it's not great for: delicate fish, eggs in a new pan (they'll stick until your seasoning is mature), and long-simmered acidic sauces.
