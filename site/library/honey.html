Honey

What it is. How bees make it. Why it lasts forever.

Honey is one of the only foods that arrives in your kitchen exactly as nature made it. Nothing added, nothing removed — just millions of flower visits concentrated into something golden. It's been found in Egyptian tombs, still edible after three thousand years. No other food works like that.

How bees make it

A honeybee visits between fifty and a hundred flowers per foraging trip. She collects nectar — a thin, sugary liquid plants produce to attract pollinators — and stores it in a special stomach. Back at the hive, she passes the nectar to house bees, who chew it, mixing it with enzymes that begin breaking complex sugars into simple ones. Then they spread it across the honeycomb and fan it with their wings to evaporate water. When the moisture content drops below about 18%, they cap the cell with wax.

That's honey. Nothing added, nothing processed. The bees did all the work.

Why it never spoils

Three things protect honey from spoiling: low water content (bacteria can't grow), high acidity (pH 3.2–4.5), and hydrogen peroxide (produced by the glucose oxidase enzyme bees add during processing). Together, these make honey inhospitable to nearly every microorganism.

Safety note: Never give honey to infants under one year old. Honey can contain Clostridium botulinum spores, which are harmless to older children and adults but can cause infant botulism in immature digestive systems.

Crystallization is normal

Crystallized honey hasn't gone bad. Some honeys crystallize faster than others — it depends on the sugar balance in the nectar the bees collected. To re-liquefy, place the jar in warm water (not boiling) and stir occasionally. Never microwave honey — it heats unevenly and destroys the delicate flavors.

Some uses are actually better with crystallized honey. It spreads on toast without dripping.

Try this: Hot Honey

The simplest recipe in the book. Pour honey over dried chile flakes in a jar. Let it sit at room temperature for a few days. Drizzle on pizza, fried chicken, roasted vegetables. The heat builds slowly and the honey keeps it rounded.
