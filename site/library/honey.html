Honey

What it is. How to use it. Why it lasts forever.

Honey is one of the only foods that arrives in your kitchen exactly as nature made it. Nothing added, nothing removed — just millions of flower visits concentrated into something golden. It's been found in Egyptian tombs, still edible after three thousand years. No other food works like that.

How bees make it

A honeybee visits between fifty and a hundred flowers per foraging trip. She collects nectar — a thin, sugary liquid plants produce to attract pollinators — and stores it in a special stomach. Back at the hive, she passes the nectar to house bees, who chew it, mixing it with enzymes that begin breaking complex sugars into simple ones. Then they spread it across the honeycomb and fan it with their wings to evaporate water. When the moisture content drops below about 18%, they cap the cell with wax.

That's honey. Nothing added, nothing processed. The bees did all the work.

Why it never spoils

Three things protect honey from spoiling: low water content (bacteria can't grow), high acidity (pH 3.2–4.5), and hydrogen peroxide (produced by the glucose oxidase enzyme bees add during processing). Together, these make honey inhospitable to nearly every microorganism. Archaeologists have opened sealed jars from Egyptian tombs and found honey that was still good.

Safety note: Never give honey to infants under one year old. Honey can contain Clostridium botulinum spores, which are harmless to older children and adults but can cause infant botulism in immature digestive systems.

Types of honey

Honey tastes like the flowers the bees visited. A clover honey is mild and universally useful. Buckwheat honey is dark and almost molasses-like — better for baking than for tea. Orange blossom honey carries a citrusy floral note that's hard to replicate with anything else.

Here are the common varietals and what they're good for:

  • Clover — Mild, classic honey flavor. Your everyday honey. Good in everything.
  • Wildflower — Varies by region and season. Usually more complex than clover. A good all-purpose honey with character.
  • Orange blossom — Light, floral, citrusy. Excellent in tea, on biscuits, or anywhere you want the honey to be noticed.
  • Buckwheat — Dark, strong, almost malty. Best in baking, barbecue sauces, and anywhere that can stand up to a bold flavor.
  • Tupelo — Light gold, buttery, slow to crystallize. From Florida and Georgia river swamps. Prized and priced accordingly.
  • Manuka — From New Zealand manuka trees. Thick, earthy, medicinal-tasting. Studied for antimicrobial properties. Very expensive.
  • Acacia — Very light, almost clear, with a mild vanilla-adjacent flavor. Stays liquid nearly forever due to high fructose content.
Is raw honey worth it? Yes. Raw honey hasn't been heated above normal hive temperatures, so it retains pollen, enzymes, and subtle flavor compounds that pasteurization strips out. It's more likely to crystallize, but crystallization is normal and doesn't mean the honey has gone bad. If you have access to local raw honey, try it side by side with a supermarket honey — the difference is noticeable.

Crystallization: it's not ruined

Crystallized honey is perfectly fine. It hasn't gone bad. Some honeys crystallize faster than others depending on their glucose-to-fructose ratio (more glucose = faster crystallization). Raw honey crystallizes more readily than pasteurized because the pollen particles give the sugar crystals something to form around.

To re-liquefy: place the jar in warm water (not boiling — high heat destroys flavor compounds). Stir occasionally. Never microwave honey. It heats unevenly, can boil over, and destroys everything interesting about the honey.

Some uses are actually better with crystallized honey: it spreads on toast without dripping, and it creams into a spreadable texture that's popular in Europe.

In the kitchen

Hot Honey

The simplest thing you'll make all year. Pour honey over dried chile flakes in a jar. Let it sit at room temperature for a few days. That's it. Drizzle on pizza, fried chicken, roasted vegetables. The heat builds slowly and the honey keeps it rounded.

Honey Butter

Soften a stick of butter. Whip in 2–3 tablespoons of honey and a pinch of salt. Roll into a log in wax paper and refrigerate. Slice off rounds for cornbread, biscuits, pancakes. Keeps for weeks.

Honey Mustard Dressing

2 parts olive oil, 1 part whole-grain mustard, 1 part honey, a splash of vinegar, salt, pepper. Shake or whisk. Bright enough for salad, sticky enough for roasted vegetables.

Honey Lemon Ginger Tea

Hot water, a spoonful of honey, a squeeze of lemon, a few thin slices of fresh ginger. The oldest remedy in the book, and still the one that actually helps a sore throat. (The honey coats, the steam opens things up, the ginger warms.)

Substituting honey for sugar

Use about ¾ cup of honey for every cup of sugar. Reduce the other liquid in the recipe by 2–3 tablespoons. Lower the oven temperature by 25°F — honey browns faster than sugar. Add ¼ teaspoon of baking soda per cup of honey to balance the acidity. Works best in quick breads, muffins, and moist cakes. Less reliable in delicate pastries or recipes where sugar does structural work (cookies, meringues).

Storage

Keep honey in a sealed container at room temperature. A cupboard is better than the fridge — cold temperatures accelerate crystallization. The one thing honey can't defend against is moisture. If water gets in, fermentation can start. Keep the lid on tight.

Honey will eventually darken and lose some aroma over very long periods, but it won't become unsafe. If it crystallizes, warm it gently. If it ferments (bubbles, yeasty smell), it's still not dangerous — but it won't taste good. Use it in baking where other flavors will cover it.
