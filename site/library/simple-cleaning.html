Simple Cleaning

Three ingredients. A few simple recipes. A rhythm that fits real life.

A clean house doesn't need a cabinet full of products. Most household cleaning can be handled with three ingredients you probably already have: vinegar, a mild soap, and baking soda. The trick is knowing what each one does — and what they don't do together.

The three-ingredient cleaning pantry

White vinegar

An acid that cuts through grease, dissolves hard water deposits, and neutralizes odors. Use it on glass, mirrors, countertops, and bathroom surfaces. Mix 1:1 with water in a spray bottle for an all-purpose cleaner. For glass, use 1 part vinegar to 2 parts water.

Honest note: Vinegar doesn't disinfect. It reduces some bacteria but won't kill pathogens the way bleach or hydrogen peroxide will. For disinfecting — when someone in the house is sick, or after handling raw meat — use a commercial disinfectant or diluted bleach. This isn't absolutism.

Mild soap

Castile soap or plain unscented bar soap grated and dissolved in warm water handles everyday cleaning: floors, walls, baseboards, the outside of appliances. Soap lifts dirt and oil that water alone can't move. A tablespoon of castile soap in a gallon of warm water is your floor cleaner.

If castile soap irritates your skin: Some people find it harsh (it's high-pH). Plain bar soaps like Ivory or Dove Sensitive work just as well. Grate a bar, dissolve in hot water, and use the same way. If you're washing by hand, wear gloves regardless — it's the water, not just the soap, that dries skin.

Baking soda

A mild abrasive that scrubs without scratching. Use it on sinks, tubs, stovetops, and anything that needs a little grit. Sprinkle it on, scrub with a damp cloth or sponge, rinse. For baked-on oven mess, make a paste with a little water and let it sit before scrubbing.

The baking soda and vinegar myth

You've seen it everywhere: mix baking soda and vinegar, watch it fizz, and somehow this cleans things. It doesn't.

What you're watching is an acid neutralizing a base. They cancel each other out and produce salt water, carbon dioxide, and a satisfying foam that does approximately nothing for cleaning. The fizz is theater.

Here's what actually works: use them separately. Scrub with baking soda first (the abrasive action does the work). Rinse. Then wipe with vinegar (the acid cuts any remaining residue). Sequential, not simultaneous. The order matters.

Five recipes that actually work

All-purpose spray

Mix 1 part white vinegar with 1 part water in a spray bottle. Use on countertops, tables, and most hard surfaces. Skip it on stone (granite, marble) — the acid can etch the surface. For stone, use soap and water instead.

Glass cleaner

Mix 1 part vinegar with 2 parts water. For streak-free results, wipe with newspaper or a microfiber cloth instead of paper towels. For genuinely filthy windows, add a drop of dish soap.

A better glass cleaner: If you want zero streaks every time with no effort, 1 part isopropyl alcohol to 2 parts water beats vinegar. It evaporates faster, cuts grease better, and leaves nothing behind. Vinegar works fine. Alcohol works better. Both are cheap.

Soft scrub

Sprinkle baking soda directly onto the surface (sink, tub, stovetop). Scrub with a damp cloth or sponge. Rinse. For extra cleaning power, add a few drops of liquid soap to the baking soda to make a paste.

Floor cleaner

One tablespoon of castile soap (or grated bar soap dissolved in hot water) per gallon of warm water. Mop as usual. No rinse needed unless you used too much soap — if the floor feels sticky, you did. Use less next time.

Oven cleaner

Make a thick paste of baking soda and water. Spread it over the interior surfaces, avoiding the heating elements. Let it sit overnight. In the morning, scrape off the dried paste with a plastic scraper or spatula, then wipe clean with a damp cloth. For stubborn spots, a little vinegar on a cloth helps dissolve the remaining baking soda residue.

Products worth buying

Some jobs are easier with a commercial product. No judgment.

  • Bar Keepers Friend — For stainless steel sinks, cookware, and anything with mineral buildup. Contains oxalic acid. It works better than baking soda for heavy-duty jobs.
  • A good enzyme cleaner — For pet messes, lingering odors, and anything biological. DIY doesn't touch this category.
  • Toilet bowl cleaner — Commercial products have cling agents and disinfectants designed for the job. Vinegar and baking soda won't get you the same result.

A cleaning rhythm, not a routine

Routines break. Rhythms survive disruption. A rhythm is a pattern you return to when you can — not a schedule that makes you feel behind.

Daily (as you go): Wipe counters after cooking. Sweep or spot-clean the floor where you eat. Do the dishes. That's it.

Weekly (pick a day): Clean the bathroom. Mop the floors. Change sheets and towels. Wipe down kitchen surfaces thoroughly. One load of laundry you actually put away.

Monthly (when you notice): Wipe cabinet fronts. Clean inside the microwave. Dust surfaces you don't use daily. Run an empty hot cycle in the washing machine.

Twice a year (spring and fall): Wash curtains. Flip or rotate mattresses. Clean behind the fridge. Vacuum under furniture. Wipe down walls and baseboards. These are the tasks nobody remembers until something smells or stops working.

Company in twenty minutes: Clear surfaces into a laundry basket (deal with it later). Wipe kitchen and bathroom counters. Sweep visible floors. Take out the trash. Light a candle if you're feeling ambitious. That's all anyone will notice.
