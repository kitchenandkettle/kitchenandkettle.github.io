     1|# Brand Strategy & Identity Guide
     2|
     3|## 1. Core Values
     4|
     5|These are the filter — every decision, piece of content, and design choice gets run through them.
     6|
     7|**Usefulness over aesthetics.**
     8|The project earns trust by working, not by looking good. Beautiful things that don't work belong elsewhere.
     9|
    10|**Simplicity as skill.**
    11|Simpler systems take more knowledge, not less. The work is in knowing enough to strip things down to what actually matters.
    12|
    13|**Durability over optimization.**
    14|"Good enough" that lasts five years beats "perfect" that breaks in one. Favor methods, tools, and recipes that prove themselves over time.
    15|
    16|**Knowledge over products.**
    17|Sell understanding, not stuff. If someone learns to make it themselves and never buys anything from you, that's success.
    18|
    19|**Honesty over persuasion.**
    20|No fear-mongering about toxins, no manufactured urgency, no invented problems to sell solutions. Say what works, admit what doesn't, let people decide.
    21|
    22|**Coherence over categories.**
    23|Food, cleaning, body care, and household rhythms belong together. Treating them as separate subjects is a marketing invention, not a real distinction.
    24|
    25|**Warmth without performance.**
    26|Calm, steady, and kind — not because it's on-brand, but because real homes run better on patience than on pressure.

    30|## 2. Audience Profile
    31|
    32|**Primary: The Quietly Overwhelmed**
    33|
    34|- Age 28–55, slight skew toward women but the project should not gender itself
    35|- Lives in a house or apartment — not acreage
    36|- Has a job, possibly kids, definitely not endless time
    37|- Feels unsettled by consumer culture but doesn't identify as a "minimalist" or "crunchy"
    38|- Tried the influencer-organized home and it didn't stick because real life doesn't look like that
    39|- Wants systems that hold up under fatigue, busy weeks, and low motivation
    40|- Curious about traditional methods but put off by the aesthetic and rhetoric around them
    41|- Possibly grew up without learning these skills and feels some grief or frustration about that
    42|- Values competence, self-sufficiency, and practical knowledge — not as identity but as quiet capability
    43|
    44|**Secondary: The Seekers**
    45|
    46|- People actively trying to reduce consumption, waste, or household complexity
    47|- People with health concerns who want simpler body care and food without the fear narrative

    49|- People leaving social media who want reliable reference material elsewhere

    53|## 3. Brand Voice
    54|
    55|**The North Star:** Talk like the most competent person you know, who is also the kindest.
    56|
    57|| Dimension     | Description |
    58||---------------|-------------|
    59|| **Tone**      | Warm, steady, calm. Not chipper. Not dry. Think: a good teacher who respects your intelligence. |
    60|| **Authority** | Earned, not claimed. "This works because I've done it a hundred times" rather than "as an expert I recommend." |
    61|| **Complexity**| Never talk down. Explain things clearly enough that a beginner can follow, without making experts feel condescended to. |
    62|| **Humor**     | Dry, occasional, never at anyone's expense. Self-deprecating about household failures is welcome. |
    63|| **Emotion**   | Honest but contained. Acknowledge that life is hard, homes get messy, people get tired — then offer something useful. |
    64|| **Gender**    | Neutral by default. If a recipe or system comes from a grandmother, say so — but don't frame the whole project as "for women." |
    65|
    66|**Voice examples:**
    67|
    68|*Bad:* "Revolutionize your morning routine with these 5 game-changing hacks!"
    69|
    70|*Good:* "Here's a breakfast system that works. It's not exciting, but it holds up on mornings when you can't think."
    71|
    72|*Bad:* "DITCH the toxic chemicals! Your family deserves better!"
    73|
    74|*Good:* "Vinegar and soap handle most household cleaning. Here's when to use each, and when you actually need something stronger."
    75|
    76|*Bad:* "Slow living in our farmhouse kitchen 🕯️🌿"
    77|
    78|*Good:* "This bread recipe takes most of an afternoon, but most of that is waiting. The actual work is about twenty minutes."

    82|## 4. Naming Directions
    83|
    84|### Direction A: Kitchen as Center of Home
    85|
    **Kitchen & Kettle** (final choice)
    Two warm, grounded nouns paired like an old shop sign. "Kitchen" anchors the domain — food, preservation, household. "Kettle" is the logo, the heat source, the symbol. Together they feel like a business that's been there a hundred years. No grammar tricks. No AI-speak. Just a name that sounds right when you say it aloud.
    88|
    89|**The Working Pantry**
    90|Practical, humble, food-forward. "Working" suggests it's in active use, not staged for photographs. "Pantry" is the hub of preservation, staples, and household supplies. Grounded and slightly old-fashioned without being precious.
    91|
    92|**Kitchen & Cupboard**
    93|Expansive rhythm that suggests the kitchen proper and everything stored around it — preserves, supplies, tools. Has the feel of a well-organized general store or an inherited recipe collection.
    94|

    96|
    97|**Common Goods**
    98|Refers to things everyone needs and knowledge everyone should have access to. Democratic, unpretentious, slightly general-store feeling. Good for eventual product or book extensions.
    99|
   100|**The Household Manual**
   101|Direct and bookish. Positions the project as reference material — something you consult, not something you scroll. Feels like a book that lives on a shelf for years.
   102|
   103|**Made to Keep**
   104|Emphasizes durability, intentionality, and preservation. "Keep" again works across food, home, and traditions. Slightly more crafted/handmade feeling.
   105|
   106|### Direction C: Single-Word Concepts
   107|
   108|**Stocked**
   109|Implies preparedness, a full pantry, a household that has what it needs. Confident and calm. Works as a domain name (stocked.com unlikely but branded variants possible).
   110|
   111|**The Keeping**
   112|Strips to the essential verb. Broader than kitchen-only. Could encompass home, food, skills, traditions. Modern-feeling while pointing at old ideas.
   113|
   114|**Hearth**
   115|The traditional center of the home — warmth, food, gathering. Warm without being feminine-coded. A single powerful word, though potentially too abstract on its own.
   116|
   117|**Kept**

   119|

   121|
   122|**Root & Rind**
   123|Earthy and food-focused without being agrarian cosplay. Root vegetables, citrus rinds, the usable parts of things. Works for a project that spans food and household. Slightly more visual/poetic.
   124|
   125|**Still Good**
   126|Double meaning: "still good" as in not yet spoiled (preservation, anti-waste) and "still" as in calm. Works beautifully as a name for a preservation-focused project. Wry, grounded, memorable.
   127|
   128|**Work & Warmth**
   129|The twin poles of a real home: the labor and the comfort, both honored. No false promise that it's all cozy.
   130|
   131|**Salt & Shelf**
   132|Preservation imagery (salt-curing, shelf-stable foods). Simple kitchen staples. Suggests both the ingredients and the place they're stored.

   221|## 9. Typography
   222|
   223|### Primary typeface: Serif for headings and display
   224|
   225|**Requirements:** Legible at small sizes, warm but not fussy, not a "farmhouse" slab serif, not a fashion-serif (no Didot/Bodoni).
   226|
   227|Options (system/Google Fonts priority):
   228|1. **Crimson Pro** — Open-source, highly readable, warm oldstyle feel without nostalgia drag. Works at display and text sizes.
   229|2. **Source Serif 4** — Warmer than Source Serif original. Clean, modern proportions with traditional warmth.
   230|3. **Lora** — Balanced, contemporary serif. Slightly more feminine than the others; use carefully.
   231|4. **Libre Baskerville** — Classic book face. Trustworthy, readable. Maybe too "literary" — test it.
   232|5. **Fraunces** — Variable font with a warm, slightly quirky character. Goes from soft display to readable text.
   233|
   234|**Recommendation: Crimson Pro** — it hits warmth, readability, and practicality. It looks like it belongs in a kitchen book from any decade.
   235|
   236|### Secondary typeface: Sans-serif for UI, labels, and small text
   237|
   238|**Requirements:** Clean, neutral, warm rather than geometric-cold. Works well at small sizes.
   239|
   240|Options:
   241|1. **Work Sans** — Approachable, slightly rounded. Good for UI and labels.
   242|2. **Public Sans** — US government design system font. Neutral, highly readable, civic rather than commercial.
   243|3. **Karla** — Friendly grotesk. Warm without being soft.
   244|4. **Atkinson Hyperlegible** — Designed for maximum legibility. Braille Institute. Inclusive and practical.
   245|
   246|**Recommendation: Work Sans** — Warm, clear, good counterpoint to Crimson Pro without clashing.
   247|
   248|### Typography system

   250|| Element | Font | Weight | Size (desktop) |

   433|## 13. Making It Feel Cohesive
   434|
   435|This is the central challenge: the project spans recipes, cleaning, body care, preservation, and household systems. Without intentional structure, it reads as an unfocused lifestyle blog. Here's how to make it coherent:
   436|
   437|### 13a. The Unifying Framework: "The Kitchen and Everything It Holds"
   438|
   439|Everything stems from the kitchen. Not because the kitchen is aesthetic, but because:
   440|
   441|- Food is preserved in the kitchen
   442|- Cleaning supplies are made from kitchen ingredients
   443|- Body care products use kitchen ingredients (oils, herbs, wax, salt)
   444|- Household systems radiate out from kitchen workflow
   445|- The kitchen is where traditional skills live
   446|
   447|The kitchen is the anchor. Everything else is a natural extension.
   448|
   449|### 13b. The Unifying Philosophy: "Works Because It Works"
   450|
   451|Every piece of content gets evaluated against the same question: "Does this actually work reliably?" If the answer is no, or if it only works under perfect conditions, it doesn't belong. This standard applies equally to a bread recipe, a cleaning routine, and a body balm.
   452|
   453|### 13c. Structural Cohesion
   454|
   455|**Cross-linking by ingredient and method:**
   456|A recipe for preserved lemons links to the preservation guide, which links to a cleaning vinegar recipe, which links to a salad dressing. The ingredient is the thread.
   457|
   458|**Recurring formats:**
   459|Every recipe post, cleaning guide, and body care tutorial uses the same template structure. The reader knows where to look for ingredients, steps, storage, and substitutions regardless of category.
   460|
   461|**The seasonal layer:**
   462|A seasonal calendar ties everything together — "What to cook, preserve, and make this month" — so the reader sees the connections.
   463|
   464|**The pantry as protagonist:**
   465|The pantry (literal or metaphorical) is the recurring image, the organizing principle, and the symbol. Stock it well, understand what's in it, use everything in it. This applies to food, cleaning supplies, body care ingredients, and tools.
   466|
   467|### 13d. Visual Cohesion
   468|
   469|- One color palette across all categories
   470|- One typography system
   471|- One photography style
   472|- Section colors are used for wayfinding (recipes = green accent, preservation = red accent, etc.) but the base palette is the same
   473|- The same line-drawing illustration style for all technique guides
   474|
   475|### 13e. Voice Cohesion
   476|
   477|- The same person is writing about bread and cleaning and body balm
   478|- The tone doesn't shift by category — it's always warm, practical, honest
   479|- If something is a personal preference rather than a universal truth, say so
   480|- The authority is earned by use, not claimed by expertise

   484|## 14. Implementation Notes
   485|
   486|### Starting points
   487|
   488|Don't try to launch everything at once. Start with:
   489|
   490|1. The library / reference material (this is the long-term backbone)
   491|2. 10–15 core recipes that teach transferable skills
   492|3. One preservation method (probably fermentation or basic canning)
   493|4. One household system (kitchen workflow or weekly rhythm)
   494|5. A "Start Here" page that orients new readers
   495|
   496|Grow outward from there. Depth before breadth.
   497|
   498|### What to avoid launching with
   499|
   500|- A blog feed sorted by date
   501|
