Start here

If you feel overwhelmed, start with these five things. They don't require special equipment, unusual ingredients, or a lot of time. They're the things that give you the most capability for the least effort.

Step one

Learn to make one bread. Not Instagram bread. The kind you make on a Tuesday because you ran out and don't want to go to the store. Once you can make bread from flour, water, salt, and time, your kitchen is never truly empty.

Browse bread recipes →

Step two

Start a perpetual stock. Keep a container in the freezer. Add vegetable scraps, bones, herb stems. When it's full, simmer it with water. You'll never buy stock again, and everything you cook will taste better. This takes five minutes a week.

Read the perpetual stock guide →

Step three

Reduce your cleaning products to three. Soap, vinegar, and baking soda handle most of a house. You might keep one commercial product for the toilet and one for laundry. That's five products total. Less to buy, less to store, less to think about.

Read the cleaning guide →

Step four

Make one body care product. Start with something unfailingly simple: an infused oil. Fill a jar with dried herbs (calendula, lavender, rosemary — whatever you have), cover with olive oil, wait two weeks, strain. Use it after showering. You just replaced lotion.

Browse body care guides →

Step five

Design a weekly rhythm that survives a bad week. Not a rigid routine. A set of patterns flexible enough to survive illness, travel, and exhaustion. The goal isn't a perfect home. It's a home that stays functional even when you're not.

Read the weekly rhythm guide →

A note on pace

You don't need to do all five. You don't need to do them in order. You don't need to do them this week.

Pick one. Try it until it feels natural — that might take a month. Then pick another if you want.

The point isn't to optimize your home. It's to build capability slowly, so that over time, your kitchen becomes a place of competence and calm instead of a source of stress and shopping lists.

One thing. Done well. That's enough.
